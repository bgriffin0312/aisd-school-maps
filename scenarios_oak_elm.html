AISD attendance scenarios: segregation, exposure, and utilization

Texas Appleseed analysis on AISD's own published building-capacity roster (Oak/Elm "Scenario 8", June 2026). Segregation/poverty on residential demographics; utilization shown on both bases. The illustrative plan is built on AISD's building capacities, with a compactness constraint (zones as tidy as the district's own Oak/Elm drafts) and feeder-pattern alignment — every elementary feeds ≤3 middle schools (most ≤2), and middle→high feeders meet AISD's cap except one school (Marshall), where Eastside HS being at capacity blocks any cleaner split. It is an existence proof, not a proposed map.

Exhibit 1 — Segregation & concentrated poverty, by level

Dissimilarity Index (DI): % of a group that would need to switch schools for an even spread — lower is less segregated. Exposure: a typical student's % White schoolmates — higher is more integrated. White isolation: a typical White student's % White schoolmates. ED = economically disadvantaged. Best value per column in red. Residential basis (who lives in each zone) — the same straight-residency yardstick AISD uses to score Oak/Elm. The italic Current — actual enrollment row is today's real student bodies (TEA 2024-25, who actually attends): a reference, not a boundary plan. It runs more segregated than the residential baseline at ES and HS because charter/magnet/transfer sorting pulls actual student bodies further apart than where families live.

Exhibit 2B — Two utilization metrics on AISD’s own Oak scenario

The same Oak boundaries, scored two ways. Residence-based (AISD’s published figure) counts students living in the zone; enrolled (our capture model) estimates who actually attends. A large positive gap (residence ≫ enrolled) is charter/transfer flight — the zone has the children but the building sits empty (e.g. LBJ); a large negative gap is magnet draw pulling students in from outside (e.g. McCallum). Amber = gap ≥ 25 points.
